Why Is My Period Late? 12 Common Reasons

A late period can be stressful, especially if your menstrual cycle is usually regular. While pregnancy is often the first thing many people think of, it is only one of several possible reasons for a delayed period. Lifestyle changes, hormonal fluctuations, certain medical conditions, and medications can all affect when your period begins.

Understanding the possible causes can help you decide whether a late period is a normal variation or a sign that you should seek medical advice.

What Is Considered a Late Period?

A typical menstrual cycle lasts between 21 and 35 days. If your period hasn't started within your expected time frame or is more than a week later than usual, it may be considered late.

12 Common Reasons Your Period May Be Late

Reason Explanation
1. Pregnancy Pregnancy is one of the most common reasons for a missed period if you are sexually active. Taking a home pregnancy test after a missed period can help determine whether pregnancy is the cause.
2. Stress Emotional or physical stress can temporarily affect hormone levels and delay ovulation, leading to a late period.
3. Significant Weight Loss Losing a large amount of weight or having a very low body weight may reduce hormone production needed for regular menstrual cycles.
4. Excessive Exercise Intense physical training, especially when combined with inadequate calorie intake, can disrupt normal menstrual cycles.
5. Polycystic Ovary Syndrome (PCOS) PCOS is a common hormonal condition that can cause irregular or missed periods due to irregular ovulation.
6. Thyroid Disorders Both an underactive and overactive thyroid can interfere with hormones that regulate the menstrual cycle.
7. Birth Control Changes Starting, stopping, or changing hormonal birth control may temporarily affect your period.
8. Perimenopause As menopause approaches, hormone levels naturally fluctuate, making periods irregular.
9. Illness Short-term illnesses such as infections or fever may delay ovulation and menstruation.
10. Certain Medications Some medications, including hormonal treatments, certain antidepressants, and chemotherapy drugs, can affect menstrual cycles.
11. Hormonal Imbalances Conditions affecting estrogen, progesterone, prolactin, or other hormones can delay menstruation.
12. Natural Cycle Variation Even healthy individuals occasionally experience a late period due to normal hormonal fluctuations.

Symptoms That May Accompany a Late Period

  • Breast tenderness
  • Mild cramping
  • Bloating
  • Mood changes
  • Acne
  • Fatigue
  • Headaches

When Should You Take a Pregnancy Test?

If you are sexually active and your period is late, a home pregnancy test is generally most accurate after your missed period. If the result is negative but your period still does not start after several days, repeating the test or consulting a healthcare professional may be appropriate.

When Should You See a Doctor?

You should consider seeking medical care if:

  • Your period is more than three months late and you are not pregnant.
  • You frequently have irregular or missed periods.
  • You experience severe pelvic pain.
  • You have unusually heavy bleeding once your period begins.
  • You have symptoms such as excessive hair growth, unexpected weight changes, or nipple discharge.
  • You suspect pregnancy but experience severe pain or heavy bleeding.

Can You Prevent Late Periods?

Not every delayed period can be prevented, but maintaining overall health may reduce the risk of irregular cycles.

  • Manage stress.
  • Maintain a healthy body weight.
  • Eat a balanced diet.
  • Exercise in moderation.
  • Get enough sleep.
  • Attend regular medical checkups.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is it normal to have a late period occasionally?

Yes. An occasional late period can happen even in healthy individuals due to temporary hormonal changes.

Can stress delay my period?

Yes. High levels of emotional or physical stress may delay ovulation, which can postpone menstruation.

How many days late is considered normal?

A variation of a few days is common. If your period is more than a week later than expected or repeatedly irregular, speak with a healthcare provider.

Can travel affect my menstrual cycle?

Yes. Travel, changes in sleep patterns, jet lag, and disruptions to your routine can temporarily delay your period.
