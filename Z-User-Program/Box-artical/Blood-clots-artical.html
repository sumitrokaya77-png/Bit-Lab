Blood Clots During Your Period? What It Means

Seeing blood clots during your period can be alarming, especially if it happens for the first time. In many cases, menstrual blood clots are completely normal and are simply part of your body's natural process. However, very large or frequent clots—especially when combined with heavy bleeding or severe pain—may indicate an underlying health condition that deserves medical attention.

What Are Period Blood Clots?

Period blood clots are thick, jelly-like collections of blood, tissue, and proteins that are shed from the uterus during menstruation. They can range from bright red to dark red or brown depending on how long the blood has remained in the uterus before leaving the body.

Small clots are common, particularly on heavier flow days.

Why Do Blood Clots Form?

Your body naturally produces substances called anticoagulants that help keep menstrual blood from clotting. During heavy menstrual flow, however, blood may leave the uterus faster than these substances can work, allowing clots to form.

This is why clots are most commonly seen during the first one to three days of a period when bleeding is usually heaviest.

When Are Blood Clots Normal?

  • Small (about the size of a pea or grape)
  • Occasional
  • Appear during heavy-flow days
  • Not accompanied by severe pain or excessive bleeding

For many people, these types of clots are considered a normal part of menstruation.

When Should You Be Concerned?

You should consider speaking with a healthcare provider if you experience:

  • Clots larger than a golf ball.
  • Heavy bleeding that soaks through a pad or tampon every hour for two or more consecutive hours.
  • Periods lasting longer than seven days.
  • Severe pelvic pain.
  • Frequent large clots every menstrual cycle.
  • Dizziness, weakness, or symptoms of anemia.

Possible Causes of Large Blood Clots

Possible Cause Description
Heavy menstrual bleeding The most common reason for large menstrual clots.
Uterine fibroids Non-cancerous growths that can increase bleeding.
Adenomyosis The uterine lining grows into the muscle wall, often causing painful, heavy periods.
Endometriosis Can cause painful menstruation and sometimes heavy bleeding.
Hormonal imbalance Changes in estrogen and progesterone can affect menstrual flow.
Pregnancy-related complications Heavy bleeding with clots during pregnancy requires immediate medical evaluation.
Bleeding disorders Certain medical conditions can affect blood clotting.

Can Blood Clots Be Prevented?

Since clots are usually the result of heavy bleeding, preventing them depends on addressing the underlying cause. Treatment options may include:

  • Hormonal birth control (when prescribed).
  • Iron supplements if anemia develops.
  • Treatment for fibroids or other uterine conditions.
  • Medications to reduce heavy menstrual bleeding.

Always consult a qualified healthcare professional before starting any medication.

Tips for Managing Heavy Periods

  • Stay hydrated.
  • Eat iron-rich foods like spinach, beans, lentils, and lean meat.
  • Track your menstrual cycle.
  • Use appropriate menstrual products for your flow.
  • Get regular medical checkups if symptoms persist.

Frequently Asked Questions

Are blood clots during periods normal?

Yes. Small blood clots during heavy-flow days are usually normal.

What color should menstrual clots be?

They may be bright red, dark red, or brown depending on how old the blood is.

Should I worry about large blood clots?

Large, frequent clots or clots accompanied by heavy bleeding, severe pain, or dizziness should be evaluated by a healthcare professional.

Can stress cause blood clots during periods?

Stress can affect hormones and menstrual cycles, but it is not considered a direct cause of menstrual blood clots.
